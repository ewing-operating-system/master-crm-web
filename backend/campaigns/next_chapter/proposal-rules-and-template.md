# Home Services M&A Advisory Proposal — Top 10 Rules + Master Template

---

## TOP 10 RULES FOR HOME SERVICES M&A ADVISORY PROPOSALS

### Rule 1: Lead With the Owner's Outcome, Not Your Credentials
The first thing the business owner sees must be THEIR goal — "You want to exit at $X and retire by [date]" or "You want to acquire 3 add-ons in the DFW market." Your credentials come later. Home services owners are operators, not finance people. They care about their outcome first.

### Rule 2: Show the Market Is Hot — With Specifics
Include real data: current EBITDA multiples (HVAC 4x-12x, plumbing 2.5x-8x, pest control 5x-10x), number of active PE platforms rolling up their trade, recent comparable transactions. This creates urgency and proves you know their industry. Generic "the market is favorable" language kills trust.

### Rule 3: Use a Tiered Fee Structure That Aligns Incentives
Standard structure for home services deals ($1M-$25M enterprise value):
- **Engagement Fee**: $5,000-$25,000 upfront (credited against success fee)
- **Monthly Retainer**: $2,500-$7,500/month during active marketing
- **Success Fee**: 5-10% for deals under $5M, 3-6% for $5M-$15M, 2-4% for $15M+ (or Modified Lehman)
- **Minimum Fee**: Always set a floor ($75K-$150K typical)

The engagement fee proves the owner is serious. The success fee proves YOU are serious.

### Rule 4: Define the Process in Phases With Timelines
Owners need to see exactly what happens and when. Use 4 clear phases:
- **Phase 1 — Preparation** (Weeks 1-6): Valuation, CIM creation, buyer list
- **Phase 2 — Marketing** (Weeks 7-14): Buyer outreach, NDAs, CIM distribution
- **Phase 3 — Negotiation** (Weeks 15-22): IOIs, management meetings, LOIs
- **Phase 4 — Closing** (Weeks 23-36): Due diligence, purchase agreement, close

Total: 6-9 months typical for home services deals.

### Rule 5: Speak Their Language — Trades, Not Finance
Replace "enterprise value" with "what your business is worth." Replace "EBITDA adjustments" with "your real profit after we add back owner perks." Replace "confidential information memorandum" with "the marketing book we create about your business." The proposal itself must be proof you can communicate with buyers AND owners.

### Rule 6: Highlight What Makes Home Services Deals Different
Your proposal must address trade-specific factors:
- Recurring revenue from maintenance/service agreements
- Technician retention and bench depth
- Licensing requirements by state/municipality
- Fleet and equipment condition
- Customer concentration risk
- Seasonality patterns
- Owner dependency / transition readiness

Generic M&A proposals that ignore these get thrown away.

### Rule 7: Show Your Buyer Universe — Be Specific
Include categories of buyers you will approach:
- **Strategic buyers**: Existing platforms in their trade (name 3-5 active ones)
- **PE-backed platforms**: Firms actively rolling up home services (name the funds)
- **Independent sponsors / search funds**: Operators looking for a first platform
- **Regional competitors**: Larger local/regional players seeking market share

Specificity = credibility. "We have relationships with 200+ buyers" means nothing. "We will approach Wrench Group, Apex Service Partners, and 12 other active HVAC acquirers" means everything.

### Rule 8: Include a Preliminary Valuation Range
Never propose without giving the owner a sense of what their business is worth. Use a range based on:
- SDE multiple (for businesses under $1M EBITDA): 2.5x-4x
- EBITDA multiple (for businesses $1M+ EBITDA): 4x-8x base, up to 12x for premium platforms
- Adjust for: recurring revenue %, owner dependency, geographic density, trade mix

This is the single most powerful section of the proposal. It makes it real.

### Rule 9: One Engagement = One Decision Maker
The proposal must clearly state who the engagement is with, what entity is being sold/acquired, and who has signing authority. Home services businesses often have partnerships, family ownership, or multiple LLCs. Ambiguity here kills deals later.

### Rule 10: Make the Next Step Obvious and Immediate
End with ONE clear call to action: "Sign this engagement letter and return it with the engagement fee by [date]. We begin Phase 1 on [date]." Not "let us know if you have questions." Not "we look forward to discussing." One action. One deadline.

## LLM AGENT INSTRUCTIONS

When populating this template, agents should:

1. **Collect these fields FIRST** (minimum viable proposal):
   - owner_name, company_name, trade_vertical
   - annual_revenue, adjusted_ebitda (or SDE)
   - engagement_type (sell-side or buy-side)
   - firm_name, advisor details

2. **Research and auto-populate**:
   - Current EBITDA multiples for the specific trade
   - Active buyers/platforms in that trade
   - Recent comparable transactions
   - Market timing rationale

3. **Calculate and derive**:
   - Valuation range (earnings × multiple range)
   - Fee scenarios at different sale prices
   - Effective fee rates

4. **Never leave a {{variable}} blank** — if data is unknown, use "[TO BE CONFIRMED]" and flag it for the human advisor.

5. **Tone**: Professional but accessible. No jargon without explanation. Written for a business owner who runs trucks, not spreadsheets.
